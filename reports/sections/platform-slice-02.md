## Messaging/Positioning
**Grade: B+**
- What's working: "Take a dollar number to the board. Not a feeling." is precise, B2B-relevant, and solves the exact problem GMs have in budget conversations. It positions Swoop as a board-meeting weapon, not just an ops tool.
- What's broken: The transition from "FIX IT" (the intervention terminal) to "PROVE IT" (the board metrics) to the new "Five core capabilities" section happens rapidly on one screen. These are actually three separate value propositions and they're competing for attention.
- Fix: Give PROVE IT its own section break with more visual breathing room. The "Five core capabilities" headline should appear on a fresh section scroll, not within the same viewport as Karen Wittman's story.

---

## Design/Visual
**Grade: B-**
- What's working: The dark green/black terminal mock-up for the "FIX IT" panel is visually distinctive and reads as real software output. The orange typography inside the terminal (member name, action items) is readable and on-brand.
- What's broken: The dark terminal on a cream background is jarring — it doesn't feel like a considered design choice, it feels like a UI screenshot dropped into a marketing page. The PROVE IT metrics panel ($32K, 9/14, $67K) uses orange numbers on white with small supporting text that is too small to scan quickly.
- Fix: Wrap the terminal in a subtle shadow and rounded corners to make it feel like a deliberate product showcase, not a raw screenshot. Increase the metric callout font sizes in the PROVE IT panel — the supporting labels ("ONE SAVE · ONE CALL," "MEMBERS RETAINED," "DUES PROTECTED") are too small relative to the numbers.

---

## CRO/Conversion
**Grade: C+**
- What's working: The Karen Wittman story ends with "Every signal sourced. Every action approved. Every outcome attributed — and a board-ready deck you forward without rewriting." This is strong copy that sets up a demo ask naturally.
- What's broken: There is no CTA after this powerful story. The Karen Wittman resolution ("Karen renewed in November") is the emotional peak of the page — and then it immediately drops to "Five core capabilities." The emotional momentum is wasted.
- Fix: After the Karen Wittman resolution paragraph, add: **"This is what Swoop does every week for clubs like yours. [See it in a 30-minute walkthrough →]"** — one sentence + one button, captures buyers at peak conviction.

---

## Trust/Credibility
**Grade: B**
- What's working: $32K / 9 of 14 members retained / $67K dues protected are specific, memorable metrics. "SIX WEEKS BEFORE NON-RENEWAL" as the scenario label gives temporal precision. The terminal showing "GM APPROVED · $6,400 dues protected · 3 taps · 0 spreadsheets" is credible operational detail.
- What's broken: "Karen Wittman, nine years" appears to be an illustrative example — real-sounding name, specific tenure, specific outcome. But there's no "real club, name changed" disclaimer. Sophisticated buyers will assume it's fictional, which undermines the metrics.
- Fix: Add beneath the Karen Wittman card: **"Composite example based on 14 similar interventions across Swoop's customer base. Outcomes vary."** If any are real and anonymized, say: **"Based on a real member intervention at a private club in [state]."**

## Mobile UX
**Grade: C**
- What's working: The two distinct panels (terminal on left, board metrics on right) contain self-contained stories.
- What's broken: On mobile, this two-column layout will stack — terminal first, then PROVE IT metrics, then Karen story. The narrative thread (fix → prove → story) may be lost when stacked vertically over 3 scroll screens.
- Fix: On mobile, reorder the stack: PROVE IT metrics first (the big numbers hook attention), then the Karen story, then a collapsed/summarized terminal mockup. This preserves the emotional arc on small screens.

## Navigation/UX
**Grade: B-**
- What's working: The orange "PLATFORM" eyebrow label on the "Five core capabilities" section maintains consistent brand navigation language.
- What's broken: The user transitions through three distinct narrative beats (FIX IT → PROVE IT → Five capabilities intro) without explicit section breaks or progress signals. The "Five core capabilities. One operating view." headline appears at the very bottom of the screenshot, suggesting the section transition is abrupt.
- Fix: Add a thin full-width divider between the FIX IT / PROVE IT section and the Five Capabilities section, with the SEE IT / FIX IT / PROVE IT pill navigation shown in "completed" state (all three filled in) before transitioning to capabilities.

## B2B Buyer Journey
**Grade: A-**
- What's working: This section completes the three-act structure (SEE IT problem → FIX IT action → PROVE IT outcome) which is the correct B2B narrative arc. Karen Wittman's story ties all three acts together in a single member journey — from signal detection to intervention to renewal. This is the strongest narrative architecture on the page.
- What's broken: The three-act structure is implicit, not labeled. A first-time visitor doesn't know they've just watched a complete "detect → act → prove" loop. The value of the structure is lost because it's not telegraphed.
- Fix: Add a brief bridge at the top of the PROVE IT panel: **"And then you close the loop."** — three words that signal to the buyer that the story has a resolution, encouraging them to read Karen's outcome.

## Copy/Voice
**Grade: B+**
- What's working: "Not one system flagged her. Together they did." is exceptional — short, punchy, and lands the core product value in 8 words. The terminal copy ("Mark — it's been a rough month. I'd like to personally comp two guest passes for your son's group this Sunday — GM") is authentic and operational.
- What's broken: "Every signal sourced. Every action approved. Every outcome attributed — and a board-ready deck you forward without rewriting." is the best summary sentence on the page but it's in small body text. It deserves to be a section-closing headline.
- Fix: Promote "Every signal sourced. Every action approved. Every outcome attributed." to a large-type section-closing statement. Make "a board-ready deck you forward without rewriting" its own line in slightly smaller text for rhythm: **"Every signal sourced. Every action approved. Every outcome attributed. / A board-ready deck you forward without rewriting."**

## Technical Credibility
**Grade: B+**
- What's working: The terminal mock-up showing `GM APPROVED · Mar-31 · $6,400 dues protected · 3 taps · 0 spreadsheets` is specific, realistic, and credible. The "rounds with, complaint aging 40, spend 223k" fields inside the terminal look like real data model outputs.
- What's broken: The terminal shows a CLI-style interface that may confuse GMs who expect a web app. The presentation suggests Swoop is a command-line tool rather than a modern web platform, which could be a technical credibility risk in the wrong direction.
- Fix: Frame the terminal explicitly: **"Swoop OS — the intelligence layer your team acts from"** as a caption above or below the terminal. This recontextualizes it as a deliberate design choice (a power-user operating surface) rather than a raw developer interface.

## Top 3 Priority Fixes for This Section
1. Add a CTA immediately after the Karen Wittman renewal sentence — this is the emotional peak of the entire page and there's no conversion mechanism. One button here will outperform the hero CTA.
2. Promote "Every signal sourced. Every action approved. Every outcome attributed." to a large closing headline — it's the best summary sentence on the platform page and it's buried in body text.
3. Add a "composite example" disclaimer to Karen Wittman — specific-sounding anecdotes without attribution undermine trust with sophisticated B2B buyers who are making $20K+ annual decisions.
