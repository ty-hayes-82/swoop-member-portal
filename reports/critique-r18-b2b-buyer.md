# B2B Buyer Journey Critique — Swoop Marketing Site (r18)

**Reviewer:** B2B Sales & Buyer Journey Expert  
**Date:** 2026-04-14  
**Buyer Persona:** Golf Club GM or COO  
**Deal Size:** $150K–$500K total cost of ownership, $499–$1,499/mo SaaS  
**Sales Cycle:** 3–6 months  
**Evaluation Framework:** TOFU/MOFU/BOFU coverage, objection handling, risk reduction, ROI evidence, peer proof, implementation clarity, stakeholder alignment

---

## Scoring Key

| Grade | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| A | Excellent B2B alignment — advances buyer through funnel |
| B | Good — works but has a fixable gap |
| C | Mediocre — present but not doing real buyer journey work |
| D | Weak — misses the moment, creates doubt or friction |
| F | Harmful — actively stalls or loses the buyer |

### home-hero.png / home-slice-00.png
**Section:** Hero — "Your club runs on four systems. None of them talk to each other."

**Grade: B+**

**Buyer Journey Strengths:**
- The headline is immediately diagnostic — it names the problem the GM already feels before they know the solution. This is strong TOFU problem framing that earns the next scroll.
- The sub-headline delivers a concrete outcome ($74K in dues) plus the two biggest procurement objections pre-empted in one line: "Live in two weeks. No rip-and-replace." These are the right de-risking signals for a first-impression moment.

**Buyer Journey Gaps:**
- The trust bar below the CTA ("Founding partner program · 300-member Pinetree CC · 28 integrations · Live in under 2 weeks") reads as product marketing, not peer proof. A GM's first question is "who else like me has done this?" — not "how many integrations exist?" The bar wastes its most credible real estate on a feature count.
- "Book the 30-minute walkthrough" is a high-commitment CTA for cold TOFU traffic. There is no lower-friction middle option (e.g., watch a 2-minute demo clip, see a sample morning brief). GMs who aren't ready to book a meeting have no on-ramp.
- No indication of club size fit. A 150-member club and a 600-member club have fundamentally different urgency levels. The hero doesn't qualify the buyer or signal who this is actually for at scale.

**Fix:**
1. Replace "28 integrations" in the trust bar with a named-club proof point: "Used by GMs at [Club Name], [Club Name], and [Club Name] — founding cohort." Even paraphrased/anonymized is better than a feature count.
2. Add a secondary CTA: "See a sample morning brief →" linking to a no-form PDF or screenshot sequence. This captures TOFU researchers who won't book a call yet.
3. Add a qualifier beneath the headline: "For private clubs with 200–800 members." Specificity builds trust with the right buyer and signals category leadership.

### home-slice-01.png
**Section:** Problem Amplification — "Most clubs are flying blind." (Three failure-mode cards: Member risk blind spot, Complaint follow-up gap, Demand vs. experience disconnect)

**Grade: A-**

**Buyer Journey Strengths:**
- The three cards mirror exactly how a GM experiences the problem — not as "I need better software" but as "James resigned and I had no warning." Buyer-language problem articulation is TOFU at its most effective.
- Each card ends with a consequence dollar figure ($22K annual dues at risk, $36K dues + F&B leakage), which anchors financial pain before any solution is mentioned. This primes the ROI calculator later.

**Buyer Journey Gaps:**
- The confidence percentages (91% confidence, 68% confidence, 64% confidence) floating over each card are unexplained. A GM reading this doesn't know if these are AI model confidence scores, survey data, or invented credibility signals. Unexplained statistics undermine rather than build trust with a skeptical enterprise buyer.
- The "Why this surfaced" micro-stat at the bottom of each card (e.g., "Engagement down 28% across six weeks") is framed as Swoop output but there's no attribution. It looks like a demo environment number posing as real proof.

**Fix:**
1. Either explain the confidence percentages in a tooltip or footnote ("Swoop's early-warning signal fires when these behavioral indicators converge — 91% of the time, resignation follows within 60 days"), or remove them. Mystery percentages are credibility killers in enterprise sales.
2. Label the "Why this surfaced" examples explicitly: "From the Pinetree CC demo environment — real system data, paraphrased." The site does this well elsewhere; apply it consistently here.

### home-slice-02.png
**Section:** Platform Overview — "Five core capabilities. One operating view." (Capability cards: Member Intelligence, Tee Sheet & Demand, F&B Operations)

**Grade: B**

**Buyer Journey Strengths:**
- Framing five capabilities under "one operating view" directly addresses the fragmentation problem stated in the hero — the narrative thread holds.
- Each capability card includes a live metric badge (6.4 wks avg early warning, 91% fill rate w/ routing, $5.7K monthly F&B upside), which moves the section from feature description to outcome demonstration.

**Buyer Journey Gaps:**
- The capabilities are listed as features, not as job-to-be-done answers. A GM doesn't think "I need Member Intelligence" — they think "I need to know which members are about to resign." The card headers are product-centric, not buyer-centric.
- There is no indication of which capability delivers value first, or what a club can expect in week one vs. month three. For a 3–6 month sales cycle, buyers need a value sequencing story, not a flat feature list.
- The metric badges use data from the demo environment but don't say so. Combined with unexplained confidence numbers elsewhere, this risks feeling like inflated marketing claims to a skeptical COO.

**Fix:**
1. Rename capability headers to buyer-outcome language: "Member Intelligence" → "Know who's about to resign — before they decide." "Tee Sheet & Demand" → "Fill every slot with the right member."
2. Add a "What you see in Week 1" sidebar or timeline callout beneath the capability grid. This converts the section from a feature catalog into an implementation promise.
3. Attribute all metric badges: "(Pinetree CC demo, real data)" — same standard as the proof section.

### home-slice-03.png
**Section:** Capability Cards continued — Staffing & Labor, Revenue & Pipeline

**Grade: B-**

**Buyer Journey Strengths:**

### home-slice-10.png / home-slice-11.png
**Section:** ROI Calculator — "What is member turnover costing your club?" (Interactive sliders: Total Members, Avg Annual Dues, Annual Turnover Rate; Output: Dues Protected chart, $74K net revenue gain, 13x ROI)

**Grade: A**

**Buyer Journey Strengths:**
- An interactive ROI calculator with real inputs (member count, dues, turnover rate) is MOFU gold. It allows a buyer to self-calculate their own business case without waiting for a sales call. For a $499–$1,499/mo product, a 13x ROI output at default settings is compelling and credible-feeling.
- The output structure (Exposure: $120K at risk → With Swoop: $80K recovered → Net: $74K gain after $5,988 cost → 13x ROI) is exactly the format a GM needs to make a board presentation. This is the best BOFU tool on the site.

**Buyer Journey Gaps:**
- The 65% early-intervention retention rate assumption is the most load-bearing number in the entire calculator — and it appears with zero attribution. Where does 65% come from? Is it an industry benchmark? A Swoop observed rate? A conservative estimate? If a CFO asks "how did you get 65%?" during a procurement review and there's no answer, the entire calculator collapses.
- The calculator only models dues retention. It does not include F&B revenue recovery, labor cost reduction, or waitlist revenue upside — all of which Swoop also claims to deliver. This makes the 13x ROI *conservative*, but a buyer doesn't know that. The headline number could be significantly higher if the model were complete.
- There is no "Share this calculation" or "Email this to my board" functionality. A buyer who wants to use this for internal championing has to screenshot it manually.

**Fix:**
1. Add a footnote: "65% early-intervention retention rate based on [X] — industry benchmark / Swoop pilot average / conservative estimate. Adjust this assumption using the slider." Make the retention rate a fourth slider.
2. Add tabs or toggles: "Dues only | Dues + F&B | Full operating impact" — let the buyer see the full value picture.
3. Add a "Download as PDF" or "Email this estimate" button. Internal champions need shareable artifacts.

### home-slice-13.png
**Section:** Founding Partner CTA — "Be one of our first ten clubs." (Hands-on Onboarding / Shape the Roadmap / Locked-in Pricing)

**Grade: B-**

**Buyer Journey Strengths:**
- The three founding partner benefits (hands-on implementation, roadmap influence, locked-in pricing for life) address three distinct buyer archetypes: the risk-averse operator (implementation support), the visionary GM (roadmap input), and the financially-minded COO (pricing protection). This is solid stakeholder-aware positioning.
- "Limited founding partner spots — early clubs get direct roadmap input" creates genuine scarcity without manufactured urgency. Roadmap influence is a real, defensible reason to act now.

**Buyer Journey Gaps:**
- "Be one of our first ten clubs" is a double-edged message. For some GMs it signals exclusive early access; for others it signals "this is an unproven product being used as a guinea pig." The page does nothing to resolve this ambiguity. A skeptical COO reads "first ten clubs" and thinks "no installed base, no proof of scale."
- There is no answer to the question: "What happens if this doesn't work in my club?" The founding partner program has no stated exit clause, guarantee, or success definition. For a high-commitment, early-stage engagement, the absence of a safety net is a significant barrier.
- The CTA "Apply for Founding Partner" implies a selective process, but there's no indication of what the criteria are, how long the review takes, or what the commitment looks like. Enterprise buyers will not submit an application to an undefined process.

**Fix:**
1. Add a "What if it doesn't work?" reassurance: "If we haven't surfaced a member risk signal in your first 30 days, we'll extend your pilot at no cost." Even a limited guarantee signals confidence.
2. Define the founding partner commitment: "3-month pilot. Your team's time: ~2 hours in week 1 for connector setup, 15 minutes/week thereafter." Specificity reduces perceived risk.
3. Replace or supplement "first ten clubs" with "founding cohort" and show 3 of 10 spots filled — social proof through scarcity framing rather than raw inexperience framing.

### Fix #1 — CRITICAL: Add a "What happens after I submit?" confirmation layer to the demo form
**Why #1:** The contact form is the most important conversion event on the site. The post-submit experience is undefined. Buyers who don't know when or who will follow up will be cold by the time the first contact is made. This is zero-cost copy work with maximum conversion impact.  
**Action:** Add under the submit button: "You'll receive a calendar link within 2 hours. Tyler or Alex will be on the call." Replace the 4-field form with a Calendly embed so buyers self-schedule.

---

### Fix #2 — CRITICAL: Move "#1 preferred Jonas Club integration partner" to hero trust bar and integrations section
**Why #2:** This is the single most defensible, trust-building credential Swoop has — and it's buried on the About page. 80% of buyers never reach it. In a market where Jonas powers the majority of target clubs, this credential alone can convert skeptics who wonder "will Swoop work with my system?"  
**Action:** Add "Official Jonas Club integration partner" as a badge in the nav bar or hero trust strip and in the integrations section.

### Fix #3 — HIGH: Add a 65% retention rate attribution to the ROI calculator
**Why #3:** The ROI calculator is the best MOFU tool on the site — but the 65% early-intervention retention rate is the load-bearing assumption of every number it produces. An unattributed assumption in a business case tool will be challenged by every CFO who reviews it. One sentence of attribution converts the calculator from a marketing toy to a boardroom artifact.

### Fix #4 — HIGH: Replace anonymous testimonials with at minimum one named-club attribution
**Why #4:** Anonymous testimonials have ~20% the persuasion power of named ones in enterprise B2B. The site currently has zero named customers. Even one club name with a GM's first name and club name changes the entire trust equation.
