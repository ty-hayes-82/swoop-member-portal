# Swoop Marketing Site — Copy & Voice Critique (r18)
**Reviewer:** B2B Copywriter / Brand Voice Strategist
**Persona:** Golf Club GM — experienced operator, skeptical of tech promises
**Date:** 2026-04-14

---

## Grading Key
- **A** — Sharp, specific, earns trust, moves the skeptic forward
- **B** — Solid but with at least one fixable weakness
- **C** — Serviceable; vague claims, weak verbs, or missed specificity
- **D** — Actively hurts conversion; clichés, jargon, or false promises
- **F** — Needs full rewrite

### `home-hero.png` / `home-slice-00.png`
**Grade: A−**

**Strengths:**
- "Your club runs on four systems. None of them talk to each other." — Concrete problem framing. The GM knows this to be true and feels it in their gut every Monday morning.
- "$74K+ in dues a year" with "Live in two weeks. No rip-and-replace." — Two objections answered before they're raised. Good.

**Problems:**
- "PRIVATE CLUB INTELLIGENCE · BUILT FOR GMs" — The eyebrow label is acceptable but "Private Club Intelligence" is still abstract category-speak. A skeptic reads this and thinks "oh, another SaaS category I don't need."
- "Book the 30-minute walkthrough" on the primary CTA is fine, but the secondary CTA "See it in action →" creates ambiguity — both feel like the same action. One needs to be decisively different.
- The social proof strip reads: "Live under 2 weeks · No rip-and-replace · 28 integrations" — "No rip-and-replace" is repeated from body copy. Don't waste proof strip real estate on a repeat.

**Rewrite Recommendation:**
- Eyebrow: `BUILT FOR GOLF CLUB GMs` (drop "Private Club Intelligence" — that's your category, not your promise)
- Social proof strip, replace "No rip-and-replace": `Reads Jonas + ClubEssential + your POS`
- Secondary CTA: Change "See it in action →" to `Watch a live club walkthrough →` — distinguishes it from a standard demo booking

### `home-slice-01.png` — "Most clubs are flying blind"
**Grade: B+**

**Strengths:**
- "Most clubs are flying blind." — Three short words. Strong. A GM who just got blindsided by a resignation will feel this.
- The three scenario cards (Member risk blind spot / Complaint follow-up gap / Demand vs. experience disconnect) are specific and narrate real daily failures, not abstract software gaps.

**Problems:**
- "Your tee sheet, CRM, and POS each hold a fragment. Nobody connects the dots until a member is already gone." — Good bones, but "connects the dots" is a mild cliché that softens an otherwise strong passage.
- Confidence percentages (71%, 68%, 64%) appear on the cards without explanation. A skeptical GM will wonder what they mean. Unexplained confidence scores look like made-up credibility.
- "Acknowledged ≠ resolved. Your complaint inbox tracks tickets, not saves." — The "≠" symbol is clever but may read as tech-speak in a context demanding plain English.

**Rewrite Recommendation:**
- Subhead: "Your tee sheet, CRM, and POS each hold a piece. None of them talk. By the time you connect them manually, the member is writing their resignation."
- Remove the confidence percentage labels entirely OR add a one-line explainer: `(AI signal confidence — how certain the system is this pattern is real)`
- Replace "≠": "Acknowledged is not the same as resolved. Your complaint inbox counts tickets. It doesn't count members saved."

### `home-slice-02.png` — "Five core capabilities. One operating view."
**Grade: B**

**Strengths:**
- "Swoop combines member behavior, demand, service, labor, and revenue so your team can act with confidence instead of assumptions." — Clear benefit statement, good scope.
- The five capability cards each show a real metric (6.4 wks, 91%, $5.7K, 223x, $251K) — this is exactly the right impulse. Numbers anchor credibility.

**Problems:**
- "Five core capabilities. One operating view." — This is a product feature headline, not a benefit headline. A GM doesn't want a view. They want fewer resignations.
- "Know who is drifting before they resign" — passive framing. "Know" is weak.
- Card subheads like "Staffing & Labor" and "Revenue & Pipeline" are functional categories, not benefit statements.
- "Staff to predicted demand, not static templates." — Good. More of this energy needed in the section header.

**Rewrite Recommendation:**
- Section headline: `Stop reacting to problems you already had the data to prevent.`
- Member Intelligence card headline: `Flag the members who are drifting — before they resign.`
- Staffing & Labor card headline: `Cover gaps before they cost you in service and margin.`
- Revenue & Pipeline card headline: `Show the board which interventions protected dues — by name.`

### `home-slice-05.png` — "Why not just..." objection cards
**Grade: A−**

**Strengths:**
- "A CRM tells you who resigned. Swoop tells you who's about to." — Best line on the entire homepage. Precise, memorable, true. Every GM has lived the pain of the after-the-fact CRM log.
- "You can build a dashboard. You can't build prediction." — Clean contrast. Strong.

**Problems:**
- "Why not just use a standalone waitlist tool?" — The subhead framing is good but the answer is long-winded: "Standalone waitlist tools fill cancelled slots — one function from one data source. Swoop gives you cross-system intelligence: which members to prioritize, what their dining and engagement patterns predict, and how to close the loop with personalized outreach. Waitlist software is a feature. Swoop is the operating layer." — This is 58 words where 20 would do.
- "Spreadsheets report the past. Swoop protects the future." — Good, but slightly cliché in the SaaS world at this point.

**Rewrite Recommendation:**
- Standalone waitlist card: "Waitlist tools fill slots. They don't know which member on that list is your highest retention risk — or that the slot they're booking is opposite their worst service experience. That's the difference between filling a tee time and protecting a membership."
- Keep "A CRM tells you who resigned. Swoop tells you who's about to." — this is A-tier, do not change.

### `home-slice-06.png` — "Six AI agents working your club — live."
**Grade: C+**

**Strengths:**
- "Watch what the agents surface, recommend, and protect in real time." — Concrete verb triad (surface, recommend, protect) is well-chosen.
- The live dashboard UI screenshot with actual agent names and recommended actions is far more convincing than generic product screenshots.

**Problems:**
- "Six AI agents working your club — live." — The phrase "AI agents" will trigger skepticism in a GM who has been burned by tech promises. "Agents" is a hot buzzword in 2025-2026 and reads as trend-chasing.
- "This panel auto-cycles through real scenarios from the Swoop OS." — "Swoop OS" is a brand name that has not been established yet on this page. Introducing it casually here feels like an inside-joke the reader isn't in on.
- The six agent names (Member Pulse, Demand Optimizer, Service Recovery, Labor Optimizer, Revenue Analyst, Engagement Autopilot) use mixed metaphor branding — "Pulse" and "Autopilot" are from different conceptual worlds.

**Rewrite Recommendation:**
- Headline: `Six specialists watching your club — around the clock.` (or: `Six specialists running in the background. One view. Every morning.`)
- Sub: "Each one monitors a different system. Together they surface the signals your team doesn't have time to find."
- Drop "Swoop OS" reference — too early to introduce sub-brand.
- Agent descriptions: Lead with outcome, not function. "Service Recovery: Drafts the callback script before you've read the complaint."

**Grade: C**

## OVERALL SITE SUMMARY

**What the site does well:**

5. One factual error (phone number) could sink a warm lead the moment they try to verify.

**Voice grade overall: B−**
Strong instincts throughout, undermined by copy-paste architecture and scattered jargon. The best lines on this site are genuinely A-tier. The site's problem is not talent — it's editorial discipline.
